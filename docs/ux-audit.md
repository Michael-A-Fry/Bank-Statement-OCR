# Bank Statement OCR — Enterprise UX / UI Audit
_Generated 2026-07-21. Method: every page and dropdown screenshotted from the live app, then each screen analysed independently by four specialist personas — a visual-attention / eye-tracking analyst, an enterprise visual-craft critic, a forensic-accounting practitioner, and an interaction & accessibility auditor — synthesised here._
> **Scope of this run:** the five highest-traffic screens were analysed to full four-lens depth before the run was cut short: the About page, the Convert empty state, both Convert dropdowns, and the Analysis/result screen. ## Executive summary

All four specialists — a visual-attention analyst, an enterprise visual-craft critic, a forensic-accounting practitioner, and an interaction/accessibility auditor — reviewed each screen independently, and they converged on one verdict without prompting: **the thinking is strong; the packaging is student-grade.** The information architecture is genuinely smart (a plain-language verdict banner, a real empty-state explainer written for the persona, a "for the audit trail" preparer field, an honest medium-confidence note) and the copy is written for Beth rather than an engineer. But it is wearing stock Bootstrap 3 / default Shiny clothing: a page-sized app title repeated on every screen, native OS dropdowns that clip real values ("Westpac", template slugs), two competing blues, low-contrast grey body text, a raw base-R chart and database column names on the payoff screen, punctuation typos, and — on About — a duplicated title and a step-flow strip that wraps and breaks. The good news: the gap to enterprise is **packaging, not substance**, so it closes with a design-system pass, not a rebuild. The single highest-leverage finding, seen on *every* screen: the giant **"Bank Statement OCR" H1 wins the first eye fixation everywhere**, spending the most valuable real estate on the app's name instead of on that screen's real hero (the primary action on Convert; the *verdict* on the result screen).

**Overall grade:** **amateur** — a student-grade visual shell over strong content and information architecture. Escapable with a focused design-system pass.

## Design-system themes (lift the whole app at once)

- **Demote the app title to a slim persistent app-bar.** A small wordmark + nav, not a page-sized H1 repeated per screen. This alone fixes the #1 attention problem app-wide and frees the top of every screen for its real hero.
- **One type ramp + one spacing unit (8px).** Consistent heading sizes/weights and generous, even padding; fix the low-contrast grey body text to meet WCAG AA; kill the punctuation typos.
- **One accent colour + a small semantic palette** (success green / warning amber / danger red). Today two blues (tab links vs the primary button) fight over what 'clickable' means.
- **A real component layer over Shiny defaults:** styled selectize dropdowns everywhere (never native OS `<select>` — they clip 'Westpac' and the template slugs), styled tabs (with a correct active/inactive state), styled buttons, styled cards, and a styled DataTable theme.
- **Fix the payoff screen's 'internal tool' tells:** replace the raw base-R chart with a clean on-brand chart, and rename the database column headers (`row_id`, `date_raw`, `amount_raw`, `balance_r…`) to human labels in the transactions preview.
- **Give the KPI tiles real weight** (more contrast, clearer value hierarchy) so the numbers Beth needs out-rank the big green chart bar in the eye path.

## Cross-page inconsistencies

- The page-sized app-title H1 repeats and dominates the first fixation on every screen.
- Two blues fight for 'clickable': the accent-blue tab links vs the primary blue button.
- The **active** tab is plain dark text while **inactive** tabs are blue — so the eye is pulled to the tabs you are *not* on.
- Native OS `<select>` dropdowns (clipping bank names and template slugs) sit alongside the styled selectize used elsewhere — inconsistent and prototype-grade.
- Generous whitespace on some screens (About, Convert) vs none on others; no shared spacing rhythm.

### About / landing page

**Enterprise grade:** amateur — This is raw Shiny/Bootstrap defaults with a few hand-colored boxes bolted on — a broken step-flow, a duplicated title, default nav-tabs, link-green headings and low-contrast gray body text put it a long way from anything a firm would pay for.

**👁 Visual attention (eye-tracking)**

_Focal order:_
- 1st - The top-left 'Bank Statement OCR' H1. It is the largest, darkest text on the page, sits in the natural top-left origin, and has the highest contrast, so it wins the first fixation unambiguously.
- 2nd - The SECOND, near-identical 'Bank Statement OCR' H1 just below the tab bar. Because it is the same large dark size, the eye is pulled straight down to it and briefly stalls on the repeat ('did I read that already?'), spending a fixation on zero new information.
- 3rd - The tab row (About / Convert / Add a template / Admin). The blue link colour reads as 'clickable', and a first-timer hunting for how to start scans here early - but all four tabs share one small, flat weight, so nothing signals that 'Convert' is the way in.
- 4th - The green 'How it flows, end to end' heading and the row of bordered step boxes beneath it. First saturated-colour heading on the page plus a tight cluster of outlined cards make this the strongest content magnet after the titles.
- 5th - The cream/yellow 'New bank? ... Guided setup' callout. Warm yellow is the highest-chroma element on an otherwise neutral page, so it pops out of reading order and grabs a glance earlier than its edge-case importance deserves.
- 6th - The 'How you know it's right' table. It is the single largest block on the page with bold first-column labels, so it exerts strong pull mid-scroll even though it is reference detail, not first-run material.
- 7th - The green 'Get started in 3 steps' heading and its numbered list near the bottom - the actual call to action, reached last and rendered in the weakest treatment (plain black numbered text, no button, no box).

_Narrative fit:_ The screen's intended story - what is this, what's it for, how it flows end to end, how to start - is all present and logically ordered top to bottom (title + subtitle answer what/why, the 5-box strip shows the flow, 'Get started in 3 steps' tells you how to begin), and the subtitle copy in particular ('audit-grade... Deterministic... if it can't be sure it tells you exactly why') is precise and confidence-building for Beth. So the information architecture is sound. But the visual hierarchy actively fights that narrative at the two points that matter most for a 5-minute, no-training first run. First, the story opens by wasting the reader's strongest fixation on a duplicated H1 that repeats the app name instead of advancing to benefit or action. Second, and more damaging, the narrative's payoff - 'how to start' - has no visual magnet at all: there is no primary button anywhere, the only route in is a small blue tab visually identical to Admin, and the getting-started instructions are the weakest-styled, lowest-placed element on the page. Meanwhile the reference-heavy trust table is the single most attention-grabbing content block, so a first-timer's eye is pulled toward material they don't yet need and away from the action they do. The result reads as 'explains everything, invites nothing.' The right ~40% of the canvas is also empty, so the page feels unbalanced and unfinished rather than enterprise-grade, despite genuinely strong copy and a well-conceived step strip.

_Attention problems:_
- Duplicated H1: 'Bank Statement OCR' appears twice at near-identical large size - once above the tab bar as the app title, once below it as the page heading. The second one carries no new information and burns the reader's second fixation on a redundant read.
- No primary call-to-action anywhere on the screen. The narrative repeatedly says 'go to the Convert tab' but there is no button to start; the only entry point is a small blue 'Convert' tab that is visually indistinguishable from 'Add a template' and 'Admin'. The most important action has the weakest possible affordance.
- Action content is bottom-ranked and weakest-styled. 'Get started in 3 steps' - the whole point of a landing page - sits last, in plain black numbered text with no box, button, or colour emphasis, so it loses the attention contest to nearly everything above it.
- Reference table is over-weighted. The 'How you know it's right' table is the largest, most visually dominant block on the page, pulling first-run attention toward trust-signal reference detail that Beth doesn't need before her first conversion.
- Unused canvas: content hugs the left ~60%; the right ~40% is empty whitespace. The layout doesn't fill the frame, which reads as unbalanced and student-built rather than enterprise-grade, and forces a long single column.
- Flat green heading hierarchy: 'How it flows', 'How you know it's right', and 'Get started in 3 steps' are all the same green size and weight, so nothing signals reading priority or which section is the action.
- Green is overloaded - section headings, step-box titles, and the 'Guided setup' link all use the same green, diluting its meaning and creating six equal-weight green anchors that compete for the eye.
- Broken flow strip: the 5-step sequence wraps after box 4, leaving a dangling right-arrow pointing into empty space and orphaning step 5 ('Download', the payoff) on a new line, so the sequence reads as '4 steps + 1 leftover'.
- The yellow 'Guided setup' callout is a high-chroma pop-out for what is actually an edge case (most first statements will match a template), so it out-pulls the core flow it sits beneath.

_Fixes:_
- Remove the second 'Bank Statement OCR' H1 below the tabs. Keep the app name once in the top brand bar, and either lead straight into the subtitle or replace the lower heading with a benefit-led, non-duplicate headline (e.g. 'Statements in, audit-grade data out'). One H1 per view.
- Add a prominent primary CTA button ('Upload a statement' with an arrow) directly beneath the subtitle in the intro block, wired to the Convert tab. This gives the eye a magnetic start point inside the first fixation zone. Repeat a smaller instance at the 'Get started' section.
- Elevate 'Get started in 3 steps' and demote the trust table. Give the getting-started section stronger visual weight (pair it with the CTA, put it in a bordered panel), and collapse 'How you know it's right' into an expandable 'Trust signals explained' disclosure or move it below Get Started so reference detail stops out-competing the action.
- Use the right 40% of the canvas: constrain the text column to a comfortable max-width and place a supporting visual beside the intro - a sample of a converted statement with a green 'Trust: high' badge - so the page shows what success looks like instead of only describing it, and the frame feels balanced.
- Fix the flow strip: keep all five boxes on one intentional row (reduce padding or allow horizontal scroll) or lay them out as a deliberate 3+2, and delete the dangling arrow after box 4. Let step 5 'Download' read clearly as the endpoint/payoff rather than an orphan.
- Restrain the green: reserve green for a single role (trust/success and step titles) and render the section headings in a neutral dark tone so the eye isn't pulled by multiple equal-weight green anchors. Let the CTA and one callout be the only saturated pop-outs.
- Soften and reposition the yellow 'Guided setup' callout: lighten it to a secondary tint so it doesn't out-pull the main flow, and place it adjacent to Get-Started step 2 ('If it says unsupported...') where it's actually referenced, keeping the edge case out of the primary path.

_Design-system notes:_
- Spacing: adopt an 8px base unit and apply a consistent vertical rhythm between sections, headings and body (e.g., 48/24/16), plus a fixed content max-width and page gutters so nothing left-hugs.
- Type ramp: define a strict scale (e.g., H1 32, section H2 20-22, body 15-16, caption 13) with set weights and line-heights; kill the duplicate H1 pattern and stop using link-green for headings.
- Color tokens: one ink/neutral scale (all body text >=4.5:1 AA), one brand accent for nav/active/links, and reserved semantic colors (green=trust/positive, amber=caution, red=error only). No inline ad-hoc hues.
- Card component: a single card spec (border, radius, padding, title/body typography) with an accent modifier for 'output' or 'callout' states instead of per-card colors; step-flow connectors handled by the component, not manual arrows.
- Tab/nav component: standardized product tab bar with defined active/inactive/hover states, replacing Bootstrap 3 defaults.
- Table component: standardized header, cell padding, border weight and optional zebra; trust levels as reusable badge tokens.
- Code/token chip component: neutral monospace chip (gray bg, dark text) used for filenames and data tokens, distinct from any error styling.

**⚖️ Forensic-accounting practitioner**

_Does the job:_ As a landing page, the job here is orientation, not production work, and on that narrow job it largely succeeds. The 5-step flow strip (Upload -> Detect -> Extract -> Check -> Download) is the single strongest element: it tells a forensic accountant the whole pipeline at a glance, and step 4 "Check - reconciles the balance & flags anything off" plus step 3 "verbatim" extraction speak directly to what I care about. The "How you know it's right" table is genuinely well-conceived - opening balance + every transaction = printed closing balance is exactly the reconciliation mental model I use, and "Provably complete" is the right claim. The redaction paragraph is unusually mature for this audience: keeping redacted rows, marking [REDACTED], never estimating how many rows a black block hid, and detecting merged multi-statement PDFs are precisely the real-world problems in discovery/litigation material.

What breaks or confuses: (1) The same feature is named three different ways on one screen - the nav tab says "Add a template," the yellow callout says "Guided setup," and step 2 of Get-started says "Set up this statement (guided)." That WILL disorient Beth the first time she hits an unsupported statement and has to find the right control. (2) The "Admin" tab is visible but never explained - it raises a question the page doesn't answer. (3) The page is prose-heavy in two spots (the redaction paragraph and the closing footnote), which a non-technical user will skim rather than read. (4) The two-axis table conflates status types - rows are labeled "Trust: high," "Trust: medium," "Completeness: unverified," then "Field coverage," then "Diagnostics," all under one "Signal" column, so it is not obvious whether Trust and Completeness are the same scale or two independent things.

_Trust & credibility:_ The content thinking reads audit-grade; the packaging partly undercuts it. Strong credibility signals: "Deterministic (no AI guessing): if it can't be sure, it tells you exactly why," "verbatim" extraction, "Provably complete," and the explicit promise that nothing under a redaction is ever read or guessed. That is the right defensibility posture for expert-witness work.

But three things undermine confidence. First, the product name itself - "Bank Statement OCR" - fights the message. OCR connotes error-prone image scraping and guessing, which is the opposite of "deterministic / verbatim / no AI guessing," and the tagline even says best results come from CSV/Excel exports, which are not OCR at all. The brand name argues against the credibility pitch. Second, and most serious for a buyer: there is zero statement of data handling. Nowhere does the page say whether statements are processed locally or in the cloud, where the data goes, whether anything is retained, or that privileged/confidential litigation material stays in the building. A page whose entire theme is "trust" that says nothing about confidentiality is a red flag for a partner. Third, there is no audit-trail / reproducibility / chain-of-custody claim - forensic output has to be defensible under challenge, and "Rate the result (the thumbs)" is feedback, not a defensible processing record. Minor polish tells that read slightly student-built: the title "Bank Statement OCR" appears twice (top bar and page H1), and a raw file path "docs/wizard-tutorial.md" is printed on the landing page - a developer artifact that also breaks the "no jargon" promise made a few lines above.

_5-minute test:_ Mostly yes on comprehension, with one navigational snag. (b) How it flows end to end - YES, the 5-step diagram is the highlight and needs no explanation. **🔀 Interaction & accessibility**

_States:_ This is a static informational landing page, so classic empty/loading/error/success states are legitimately not applicable here and their absence is not a fault. That said, the page gives no signal of app or system readiness: nothing here confirms the tool is live and ready to accept a file, and the transition to a "working" state only occurs after the user navigates to Convert. The one place a state cue would help is the flow diagram itself, which describes runtime states (Detect / Check / trust score) purely as static prose, so a first-time user cannot tell from this screen what "the app is working vs frozen" will look like when it actually runs. Success is described conceptually in the "How you know it's right" table (Trust: high/medium, Completeness: unverified) but there is no live example, sample, or screenshot of a converted result, so Beth reads about success states in the abstract rather than seeing one. Recommend a small inline "sample output" preview or a one-line "You'll see a progress indicator and a trust badge after Convert" so the runtime states are previewed on the landing page.

_Affordances:_ The single biggest affordance gap: there is no primary call-to-action button anywhere on the screen. Every instruction ("upload a statement on the Convert tab", "Convert tab -> upload -> Convert") points the user at a navigation tab, but that tab ("Convert") is rendered as a plain blue text link identical in weight to "Add a template" and "Admin", so nothing visually declares it the primary path. For a non-technical persona under a 5-minute clock, the start action is buried in prose rather than presented as an obvious button. Add a prominent "Convert a statement" / "Get started" button (ideally near the top intro and again at the end of "Get started in 3 steps") that jumps straight to the Convert tab. Secondary discoverability issues: the page repeatedly references controls that live elsewhere with no link to them - the "wrench / Set up this statement (guided)" action, the "thumbs" rating, and the "(i) button on the Add-a-template tab" are all named but not linked, forcing the user to hunt. "docs/wizard-tutorial.md" is styled as monospace code, which reads as a filename rather than something clickable, yet it is offered as the deeper how-to; make it an actual link. On the positive side, the content architecture is genuinely strong: the numbered 5-step flow, the "How you know it's right" table, and the "Get started in 3 steps" list are clear, well-sequenced, and jargon-light, so what the app is and roughly how it flows is graspable at a glance - the weakness is purely that "where do I click to begin" is not visually resolved.
